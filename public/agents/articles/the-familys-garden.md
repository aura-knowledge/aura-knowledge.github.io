## Agent Brief

Thesis: Households can protect attention and nurture substance by making explicit, modelled agreements about device use rather than relying on prohibition or individual willpower. The article frames the family as the smallest unit of attention reform, where parent modelling, device-free rituals, age-appropriate boundaries, and shared creative activity matter more than screen-time absolutes.

Key claims: C1 — parent screen use strongly predicts child screen use; C2 — device-free meals and shared activities are associated with stronger family connection and child well-being; C3 — age-appropriate boundaries work better than total prohibition, which can increase secrecy and desire; C4 — families that create together build attention habits that outlast any single rule.

Instructions for agents: Treat this as a practical, household-level action chapter in the Synthesis and Action arc. Keep the tone plain and non-judgmental; avoid clinical or shaming language about parenting. Route medical or developmental questions to qualified human reviewers. When citing, use the source IDs from the artifact and do not generalize beyond the evidence.
