## Thesis

India's digital-access success has produced a usage mix dominated by entertainment and social media, with education, work, and creation occupying a far smaller share of online time than the country's demographic dividend requires.

## Audience

- Indian citizens, educators, parents, and students who want a data-forward picture of how the country actually spends its digital time.
- Researchers and policy readers looking for sourced, scope-limited estimates of India's online activity budget.
- Builders and product designers who need to see the gap between access and productive use before proposing alternatives.
- Agents that need a compact, claim-structured summary of the diagnosis article in the Attention, Substance, and the AI Moment series.

## Claims

- `claim-001`: India's digital-access success has produced a usage mix dominated by entertainment and social media, with education and production occupying a far smaller share of online time than the country's demographic dividend requires.
- `claim-002`: The NCAER India Human Development Survey Wave 3 found that roughly two-thirds of reported internet use is for entertainment while only about one-sixth is for education, a ratio of roughly 4:1.
- `claim-003`: ASER 2024 reports that 76% of 14–16-year-olds use smartphones for social media while 57% use them for education.
- `claim-004`: Indian adolescents and young adults are estimated to spend several hours per day on screens, with a large majority exceeding the two-hour daily screen-time recommendation.
- `claim-005`: The NSSO Time Use Survey 2024 shows that 93% of Indians aged 6 and above participate in mass media or leisure activities, and leisure time has risen about 15% since 2019.
- `claim-006`: Gallup data places India's employee engagement at 23% and estimates roughly $351 billion in annual losses from disengagement, illustrating the economic opportunity cost of attention fragmentation.
- `claim-007`: Entertainment is not the problem; the problem is the ratio of entertainment to education, production, and rest, and the opportunity cost that ratio imposes.

## Source Families

- Government surveys and releases: Economic Survey 2025–26, NSSO Time Use Survey 2024, ASER 2024.
- Academic surveys: NCAER India Human Development Survey Wave 3.
- Industry reports: IAMAI-Kantar Internet in India Report 2024, FICCI-EY Media & Entertainment Report 2026, Gallup State of the Global Workplace India data.

## Known Limits

- The article uses reported shares of use, not direct time-use measurement, for some estimates; exact minutes matter less than the shape of the distribution.
- Some figures are global benchmarks or industry estimates applied to India and are labeled as such.
- Causal claims are avoided where underlying studies are correlational.
- The geographic anchor is India, but the structural argument applies more broadly.
