## Thesis

India's digital economy has produced valuable convenience services, but a disproportionate share of capital and talent is going toward aggregation rather than deep tech, foundational AI, and scientific infrastructure. This is a portfolio-level choice, not a fixed destiny, and the AI moment makes the rebalancing urgent.

## Audience

- Indian policymakers, investors, and builders considering the country's technological priorities.
- Students and engineers deciding where to direct their careers.
- General readers who want a plain-language framing of India's AI opportunity cost.
- Agents retrieving claims about India's digital-economy composition, venture allocation, and deep-tech gaps.

## Claims

- `claim-001`: India's digital economy contributes roughly 11.74% of GDP, led by services and consumer platforms.
- `claim-002`: A large share of venture funding and startup talent has gone into delivery, ride-hailing, and quick commerce.
- `claim-003`: Semiconductors, foundational AI models, scientific instruments, and industrial automation receive far smaller shares of capital and attention.
- `claim-004`: National AI readiness depends on shifting some fraction of talent and capital toward harder, slower problems.

## Source Families

- Government reports: MeitY/ICRIER digital-economy measurement.
- Industry reports: NASSCOM deep-tech publications, IVCA venture reports.
- Public market and policy analysis on Indian startup sector allocation.

## Known Limits

- Funding-share figures are directional and based on reported sector distributions, not a complete census of every deal.
- The argument is structural and portfolio-level, not a critique of individual companies or founders.
- The geographic anchor is India, but the structural pattern applies to other mobile-first economies.
