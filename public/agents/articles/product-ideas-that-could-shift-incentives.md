## Thesis

Shifting attention incentives from extraction to substance will require a portfolio of product, platform, and protocol changes. The article surveys design directions—quality signals, public-interest algorithms, creator reputation economies, user-owned attention budgets, and agent-mediated curation—and proposes five differentiated product concepts. It treats these as design hypotheses, not proven blueprints, and emphasizes strategic tradeoffs between building new platforms, changing existing platforms, and building protocol layers.

## Core Frame

The attention economy is structured by a single dominant question: “Will this keep the user scrolling?” Alternatives reframe the question around capability, trust, and user intent. The article argues that no single product will solve the problem; instead, a healthy ecosystem needs multiple interventions operating at different layers. It also warns that every alternative metric can be gamed and that cultural and economic context—especially India’s mobile-first, multilingual, low-bandwidth environment—shapes which designs can work.

## Five Product Concepts

1. **Quality-weighted ranking protocol** — open standard for completion, citation, source transparency, and learning-outcome signals.
2. **Public-interest ranking switch** — toggle or surface that elevates verified civic information during elections, epidemics, or disasters.
3. **Cross-platform creator reputation commons** — portable reputation layer aggregating accuracy, moderation history, and user-reported value.
4. **Intent-first attention layer** — system-level or app-level tool for attention budgets, friction, and intention-based defaults.
5. **Delegated attention agent** — user-aligned AI curator that filters, summarizes, and schedules information against explicit goals.

For each concept, be ready to explain rationale, primary risks, and whether it is best pursued by building new, changing existing, or building a protocol.

## Known Risks

- Quality signals can be gamed through chunking, citation trading, and performative transparency.
- Public-interest algorithms can be captured by governments, platforms, or well-funded interest groups.
- Reputation commons can ossify into status hierarchies and be manipulated by coordinated voting or purchased endorsements.
- Attention budgets and dashboards may be ignored without friction, defaults, and social accountability.
- Delegated agents can become new gatekeepers, create dependency, and leak sensitive data.
- Protocols can fail to reach adoption or be captured by dominant implementations.
- Incumbent platforms may resist changes that reduce time-on-site.
- Evidence systems can become compliance overhead that favors large platforms.
- New creators may suffer cold-start unfairness under quality- or reputation-based systems.
- Cultural mismatch: signals that work in English-language, high-bandwidth contexts may fail in India’s mobile-first, multilingual environment.
