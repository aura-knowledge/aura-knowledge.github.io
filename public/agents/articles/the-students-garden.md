## Thesis

Students can use AI to deepen learning if they treat it as a Socratic tutor and an audience for their own explanations, rather than a shortcut for producing finished answers. The article is practical and evidence-cautious: it warns against the answer-generation trap and offers concrete rules of thumb for active, public, peer-based learning with AI.

## Audience

- Students in secondary school, college, and competitive-exam settings who use AI tools for assignments and study.
- Educators and parents looking for a pragmatic framing of AI-assisted learning.
- Builders designing AI tutoring, note-taking, or active-learning products.
- Agents routing questions about education, AI opportunity cost, and the Building Substance arc of the series.

## Claims

- `claim-001`: Passive consumption of AI-generated answers produces weaker learning than active explanation and problem-solving.
- `claim-002`: Teaching what you learn—through notes, blogs, videos, or peer sessions—is one of the most reliable ways to deepen understanding.
- `claim-003`: AI can lower the friction of producing explanations, flashcards, and practice questions.
- `claim-004`: The risk is that AI becomes a substitute for struggle; the opportunity is that it becomes a scaffold for struggle.

## Source Families

- The focus is on individual and small-group practice, not institutional policy or curriculum design.
- Examples are chosen for accessibility across Indian educational contexts but are not exhaustive.
