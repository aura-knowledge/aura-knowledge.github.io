## Thesis

Attention habits compound across career stages: early fragmentation erodes the deep-skill foundation that later-stage autonomy and impact depend on, while small deliberate habits of depth produce widening returns over time.

## Audience

- Primary: workers and students planning long-horizon skill development.
- Secondary: parents and policy readers tracking how work attention norms spill into family and civic life.

## Claims

- `claim-001`: Early-career attention habits compound over time: fragmented focus produces a thinner skill base, while small habits of depth build capabilities that widen later options.
- `claim-002`: Complex professional skills require sustained, focused practice; the default digital work environment makes this harder by design.
- `claim-003`: Workplaces often reward responsiveness and visibility more than deep output, which can steer mid-career advancement toward people who mastered communication signals rather than core skills.
- `claim-004`: Work-related attention fragmentation spills into family and community life, weakening the relationships and social participation that support well-being over the life span.
- `claim-005`: Adult attention habits are transmitted to children, so the career-stage choices of one generation become the default environment of the next.
- `claim-006`: Small, sustained habits of depth early in a career produce widening returns in later stages, including autonomy, reputation, and the ability to choose meaningful work.
- `claim-007`: Changing attention defaults at work and home—through protected focus time, response norms, and modeled behavior—can redirect compounding toward skill and relationships rather than fragmentation.

## Source Families

- Deep-work and skill-acquisition literature (Newport).
- Workplace engagement surveys (Gallup, global workplace series).
- Digital-boundary and right-to-switch-off studies.
- Indian time-use data (NSSO Time Use Survey) and parent screen-time surveys (LocalCircles).

## Agent Involvement

Route evidentiary questions about workplace attention and productivity to the cited research. Distinguish between individual attention management and organizational design choices. Point readers to related life-phase and substance-building articles for practical next steps. Avoid prescribing one-size-fits-all schedules; acknowledge that different roles require different response norms.

## Recommended Queries

- "What evidence would strengthen claim-003 on responsiveness-vs-output promotion incentives?"
- "What counterarguments should be recorded for the compounding-habits thesis (e.g., adaptive advantages of responsiveness)?"

## Known Limits

The compounding mechanism is argued by analogy and career-stage reasoning, not by longitudinal attention-habit cohorts; source base mixes global surveys with Indian time-use data.
