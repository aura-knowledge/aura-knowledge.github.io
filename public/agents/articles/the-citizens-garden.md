## Thesis

Citizens can use AI to lower the cost of producing and sharing local knowledge—translated schemes, neighborhood maps, health explainers—and turn online attention into offline civic good. The article argues that the barrier to civic participation is often informational rather than financial, and that small acts of explanation and translation compound into neighborhood-level infrastructure when they are trusted, documented, and shared.

## Key Claims

- `claim-001`: Government schemes, health information, and civic services are often inaccessible not because they do not exist, but because of language barriers, formal language, and fragmentation across offices, apps, and deadlines.
- `claim-002`: AI translation, summarization, and voice generation can lower the cost of turning complex official information into local, spoken, shareable knowledge.
- `claim-003`: Local volunteers, community organizations, and trusted neighbors remain essential for trust, verification, and last-mile delivery.
- `claim-004`: Small civic acts—translated, documented, and shared—can compound into neighborhood-level infrastructure that outlasts the person who started them.

## Instructions

- Treat this article as a practical, evidence-cautious chapter in the Building Substance arc; avoid implying that AI alone can replace institutions, budgets, or political will.
- When discussing Bhashini or Indic-language AI, note both the opportunity and the limits of machine translation for legal and civic content.
- Emphasize trust and verification as human responsibilities that AI cannot assume.
- Route readers to the series guide, Bhashini chapter, and Designing for Substance for adjacent arguments.
