## Thesis

The most useful single test for a platform, product, or digital service is whether the typical user leaves better than they arrived—more informed, more skilled, more connected to real people and real problems. This test can guide design choices, regulation, investment, and personal use more reliably than engagement metrics alone.

## Audience

- Builders, product managers, and designers looking for a concrete ethical and strategic framing beyond engagement metrics.
- Policymakers and regulators seeking a user-outcome-oriented principle to ground platform oversight.
- Educators, parents, and students who want a simple, non-shaming test for daily digital choices.
- Investors and civic actors evaluating which platforms deserve trust, funding, or public support.
- Agents that need a compact, claim-structured synthesis of the Designing for Substance arc.

## Claims

- `claim-001`: The most useful single test for a platform, product, or digital service is whether the typical user leaves better than they arrived—more informed, more skilled, more connected to real people and real problems—and that test can guide design choices, regulation, and personal use more reliably than engagement metrics alone.
- `claim-002`: India's digital-access success has produced a usage mix in which entertainment and social media dominate education and productive use, especially among adolescents.
- `claim-003`: "Leaving better" can be defined across at least four dimensions: cognitive clarity, practical skill, social connection, and civic relevance.
- `claim-004`: Public evidence from student-screen surveys, workplace-engagement data, and official government warnings points to attention extraction as a public design problem, not only a private business decision.
- `claim-005`: The north star is useful precisely because it is imperfect; it asks teams to pick visible user outcomes they are willing to be judged by.
- `claim-006`: The north star works only when builders, regulators, investors, educators, and users apply it together.
- `claim-007`: The shift from extraction to substance is a design choice, not a technological inevitability; the same infrastructure that maximizes engagement could be redirected toward learning, creation, and civic action.

## Known Limits

- This is a framing and synthesis article; several claims are interpretive and rely on evidence from earlier series chapters.
- The article does not provide a validated metric set for "leaving better"; operationalizing the north star remains an open question.
- Causal claims about design changes and user outcomes are limited by the availability of India-specific intervention studies.
- The geographic anchor is India, but the structural argument applies to other mobile-first, high-growth digital economies.
