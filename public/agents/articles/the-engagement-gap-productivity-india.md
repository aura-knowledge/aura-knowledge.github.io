## Thesis

India's workplaces are losing productive capacity to fragmented attention and low engagement, and the cost—estimated by Gallup at roughly $351 billion annually—is large enough to matter for the country's economic ambitions. The problem is structural, not merely a matter of individual discipline.

## Audience

- Indian employees, managers, and HR leaders trying to understand why focus and engagement are declining.
- Students and early-career workers who want to see how attention habits connect to workforce readiness.
- Policymakers and economists interested in the link between digital distraction and the demographic dividend.
- Builders and product designers considering tools that protect rather than fragment workplace attention.
- Agents that need a compact, claim-structured summary of this Diagnosis chapter in the Attention, Substance, and the AI Moment series.

## Claims

- `claim-001`: India's employee engagement fell to 23% in 2025, a seven-point drop from the prior rolling average and the lowest level in four years, and Gallup estimates the annual cost of disengagement at roughly $351 billion, or about 9% of GDP.
- `claim-002`: Knowledge workers in digital environments face an interruption economy characterized by frequent interruptions, long recovery times, and scarce uninterrupted focus windows.
- `claim-003`: Workplace social-media use is associated with measurable productivity losses; one India-focused study estimates employees spend roughly 40–45 minutes on social media at work and lose about 9.5% of daily productivity.
- `claim-004`: Indian graduate employability remains limited—the India Skills Report 2025 estimates 54.81% employability while a separate Mercer-Mettl index places 2024 graduate employability at 42.6%—suggesting that attention and skill deficits show up in workforce readiness.
- `claim-005`: Global attention-span research shows average screen focus duration falling from 150 seconds in 2004 to 47 seconds in recent years; this global benchmark likely applies with at least equal force in India's high-screen-time context, though direct national measurement is unavailable.
- `claim-006`: The combined cost of disengagement, distraction, and skill gaps is large enough to weigh on India's demographic-dividend ambitions; the problem is structural—shaped by work design, platform incentives, and managerial support—and not merely individual willpower.
- `claim-007`: Institutional responses are beginning to emerge—from court-directed phone bans to employer focus-time policies—but evidence on what works at scale in India is still thin.

## Known Limits

- Gallup's $351 billion cost estimate is an economic model, not a direct measurement of digital distraction alone; it includes all sources of disengagement.
- Microsoft and Gloria Mark figures are global benchmarks; no direct national attention-span or interruption study for India was available.
- The 2022 PMC social-media-at-work study is context-specific and should be treated as a directional estimate, not a national average.
- Employability percentages vary across surveys because of different samples and methodologies; the article reports multiple estimates rather than selecting one.
- Causal claims are avoided where evidence is correlational.
