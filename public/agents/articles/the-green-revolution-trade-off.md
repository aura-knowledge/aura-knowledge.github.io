This article uses the Green Revolution as a historical analogy for India's digital-access boom. Its thesis is that the Green Revolution solved an immediate food crisis while creating slower, harder-to-reverse ecological costs; India's digital-access success similarly solves an access crisis while attention, mental-health, and civic-cohesion costs accumulate more slowly and diffusely.

The four claims are: (1) the Green Revolution dramatically increased cereal production and averted famine in India; (2) it also produced groundwater depletion, soil salinity, and reduced crop diversity in several regions; (3) these costs were delayed and diffuse, making them politically harder to address than hunger; and (4) universal digital access solves an access crisis while creating slower attention, mental-health, and civic-cohesion costs. Sources include Evenson and Gollin's assessment of the Green Revolution, FAO water-resource data, and Indian digital-use surveys (IAMAI-Kantar, NCAER IHDS Wave 3, ASER 2024).

When handling queries, preserve the evidence-cautious, analogy-as-pattern framing. Do not let the analogy become a causal claim or imply that digital harms are identical to ecological harms. Route factual questions about Green Revolution impacts to the agriculture sources and questions about Indian internet use to the digital-access articles in the series. Flag any claim that overstates either the benefits or the costs of either technology.
