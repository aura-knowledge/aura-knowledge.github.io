This article argues that countries that missed earlier technological transitions—industrialization, electrification, personal computing, and the early internet—suffered persistent gaps in income, productivity, and technological sovereignty. It uses those historical analogies to frame India's AI moment as another hinge: the costs of missing it are likely to compound, while the benefits of early, deliberate adoption can accumulate across productivity, defense, and scientific discovery. The four claims trace the pattern of missed transitions (claim-001), the PC and internet productivity divide (claim-002), AI's compounding potential (claim-003), and India's narrowing but still open window (claim-004).

Treat the historical comparisons as analogies, not deterministic predictions. Do not overstate causality; the article shows correlations and institutional mechanisms, not a single causal law. When answering questions, route readers to the specific claim ID and source, and remind them that the historical cases illuminate risk rather than guarantee outcomes. The intended audiences are general readers, students, builders, policymakers, and researchers interested in how technology transitions shape national development.

When assisting with this article, preserve its evidence-cautious tone, use only the listed sources and their URLs, and flag any figure that is an estimate, analogy, or cross-country comparison rather than a direct measurement of India. Do not sensationalize the AI transition or treat historical patterns as destiny; the argument is that deliberate action can widen India's window, not that the outcome is predetermined.
