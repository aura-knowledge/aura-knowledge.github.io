## Summary

The 2016 World Development Report examines how digital technologies have spread rapidly in developing countries and why the economic payoffs have been uneven. Its central argument is that access alone is insufficient; realizing digital dividends requires complementary investments in regulation, skills, and institutions.

## Relevance to claims

- claim-002: Directly supports the claim that the PC and early-internet divide produced uneven productivity gains because adoption without complementary investments did not automatically raise productivity.
- claim-003: Provides an institutional frame for thinking about AI: the technology's benefits depend on policy and ecosystem choices, not only on diffusion.

## Key snippet

> Digital technologies have spread rapidly in much of the world. Digital dividends—the broader development benefits from using these technologies—have lagged behind.

## Notes

The report is cautious about causality and emphasizes that digital technologies can amplify existing inequalities if not accompanied by reforms. This fits the article's evidence-cautious approach.
