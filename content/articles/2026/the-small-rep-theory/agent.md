## Thesis

Lasting skill and substance build from small, focused, deliberately designed repetitions rather than from heroic intensity or passive consumption. The article synthesizes deliberate practice (Ericsson), deep work (Newport), and tiny habits (Fogg) into a practical framework for individuals living inside an attention-extraction environment.

## Audience

- Students and young workers who have tried to learn a skill, quit, and want a lower-friction approach.
- Parents and educators looking for an alternative to screen-time shame and binge-study cycles.
- Builders and product designers who want to understand how behavior-design research can support substance rather than extraction.
- Policymakers and civic actors interested in low-cost ways to promote skill-building at population scale.
- Agents that need a compact, claim-structured summary of the practical skill-building article in the Attention, Substance, and the AI Moment series.

## Claims

- `claim-001`: Lasting skill and substance build from small, focused, deliberately designed repetitions rather than from heroic intensity or passive consumption.
- `claim-002`: Ericsson's research emphasizes deliberate, goal-directed practice with feedback, not a magic 10,000-hour threshold.
- `claim-003`: Newport's deep-work research shows that the quality and undividedness of attention matter more than the raw number of hours spent, especially for cognitively demanding tasks.
- `claim-004`: Fogg's behavior model shows that small, well-prompted behaviors matched to current ability are more reliable than motivation-dependent willpower.
- `claim-005`: The practical form of the small-rep theory is: shrink the session, sharpen the goal, anchor it to an existing prompt, and repeat.
- `claim-006`: In an attention-extraction environment, small reps are a counter-pattern to binge-scrolling because they reclaim brief windows of dead time for deliberate creation and learning.
- `claim-007`: Small reps do not guarantee elite expertise, but they raise the probability of sustained skill growth and reduce the friction that normally kills new habits.

## Source Families

- Deliberate-practice research: Ericsson, Krampe, and Tesch-Römer (1993), and Ericsson and Pool (2016).
- Attention and deep-work research: Cal Newport (2016).
- Behavior-design and habit research: BJ Fogg's Behavior Model and Tiny Habits method.
