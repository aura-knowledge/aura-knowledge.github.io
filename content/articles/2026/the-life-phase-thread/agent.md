## Thesis

The attention economy does not affect Indians uniformly; it imposes distinct harms at each life stage, and those harms compound across generations.

## Audience

- Indian parents, educators, and students looking for a life-stage map of how screens shape childhood, study, and relationships.
- Young workers and workplace designers interested in attention fragmentation and engagement data.
- Policymakers and civic actors concerned with intergenerational digital inclusion and elderly exclusion.
- Researchers and agents needing a compact, claim-structured summary of the Historical and Human Frames arc.

## Claims

- `claim-001`: The attention economy does not affect Indians uniformly; it imposes distinct harms at each life stage, and those harms compound across generations.
- `claim-002`: Heavy recreational screen use begins in childhood, with nearly half of urban parents reporting children 9–17 spend three or more hours daily on social media, video, and games.
- `claim-003`: Indian students face a split-screen adolescence in which recreational smartphone use outpaces educational use and academic anxiety is reported by a large majority.
- `claim-004`: India's young workers enter labour markets where engagement is low, attention is fragmented, and digital distraction imposes a large economic cost.
- `claim-005`: Heavy smartphone use is associated with harm to marital relationships and a weakening of community participation, suggesting the attention economy extracts from social fabric as well as individual time.
- `claim-006`: Older Indians are largely left out of the digital infrastructure, with roughly nine in ten older adults digitally excluded.
- `claim-007`: The harms of attention extraction are connected across generations, so solutions that only fix one life stage are likely to leak back into the others.

## Source Families

- Education and childhood: ASER 2024 National Findings; LocalCircles 2025 parent survey.
- Student mental health: NCERT 2022 student mental-health survey (Ministry of Education parliamentary release).
- Workplace: Gallup State of the Global Workplace 2025 India country-level data.
- Family and social fabric: vivo Switch Off Study 2022; NSSO Time Use Survey 2024.
- Elderly digital exclusion: LASI-based comparative analysis (MDPI 2024) and NITI Aayog Senior Care Reforms position paper.

## Known Limits

- Some figures are self-reported or industry-funded; they are used as directional signals, not national censuses.
- Global interruption and focus-session data are cited as benchmarks, not India-specific measurements.
- Causal claims are avoided where the underlying evidence is correlational.
- The article maps patterns rather than prescribing interventions for each life stage.
