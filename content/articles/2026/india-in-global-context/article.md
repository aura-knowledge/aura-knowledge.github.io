<p class="article-kicker">Attention, Substance, and the AI Moment · Part 12</p>

India is often described as the world's largest open internet market. That scale makes its attention economy hard to ignore. But the mechanics behind it are not Indian. Autoplay, infinite scroll, variable rewards, and algorithmic recommendations were perfected elsewhere and imported on cheap data plans. India is a high-resolution version of a global story.

<span id="claim-001" class="claim-marker" data-claim="claim-001">Claim C1</span> India ranks among the highest countries for daily smartphone screen time.

The question is not whether India has a problem. The question is whether India's size, youth, and regulatory ambition give it a special role in solving it.

<h2 id="the-scale-makes-it-visible">The Scale Makes It Visible</h2>

India had roughly 886 million internet users and about 650 million smartphone users in 2024. DataReportal's global estimates place Indian users among the highest in daily hours on mobile devices, often above four hours per day. The exact rank shifts by survey and methodology, but the direction is consistent: India sits near the top of global screen-time distributions.

![Estimated daily smartphone screen time by country](/images/articles/2026/attention-substance-ai-moment/global-screen-time.svg)

*Estimated daily smartphone screen time by country. India sits near the top of global distributions alongside other mobile-first markets. Source: DataReportal Digital 2025, ITU Facts and Figures 2024.*

This is partly because India went mobile-first and, in many households, mobile-only. Personal computers never reached most Indians; smartphones did. The Jio-era collapse in data prices turned access into habit. The result is that a large, young, price-sensitive population lives inside the same engagement architecture that operates in San Francisco, São Paulo, and Jakarta.

The scale matters because it makes the trade-offs visible. A few percentage points of attention shifted from entertainment to learning would translate into hundreds of millions of hours. A regulatory choice made for a billion users can reshape global product norms.

<h2 id="the-same-playbook-cheaper-data">The Same Playbook, Cheaper Data</h2>

<span id="claim-002" class="claim-marker" data-claim="claim-002">Claim C2</span> Brazil, Indonesia, and Nigeria show similar patterns of mobile-first entertainment and platform concentration.

These countries followed a similar script. Mobile connectivity leapfrogged fixed-line infrastructure. Entertainment, messaging, and short-form video became the default uses. A small number of global platforms captured most of the attention. The ITU notes that lower-middle-income countries have driven recent internet-user growth, and the consumption mix in these markets skews heavily toward social and entertainment rather than productivity.

The common factor is not culture. It is economics and design. When data is cheap, devices are affordable, and platforms are optimized for time-on-site, the same behavior emerges. Local content fills the feeds, but the extraction playbook is imported. The languages change; the variable-reward loops do not.

This matters for how India thinks about solutions. A policy or product design that works in India is likely to work in other mobile-first, price-sensitive markets. Conversely, models designed for wealthy, PC-era societies may not transfer cleanly.

<h2 id="the-platform-powers-are-not-exempt">The Platform Powers Are Not Exempt</h2>

<span id="claim-003" class="claim-marker" data-claim="claim-003">Claim C3</span> The US and China have larger domestic platform ecosystems but face similar debates about youth screen time and platform accountability.

American firms wrote the attention-extraction playbook. Chinese firms refined recommendation algorithms and super-app engagement at massive scale. Both now face public debates over youth screen time, mental health, and platform accountability.

The United States has moved toward Section 230 reform, state-level age-appropriate design laws, and Federal Trade Commission actions against dark patterns and data practices. China has imposed gaming limits for minors and content-recommendation regulations. Neither has solved the problem, but both show that even the largest ecosystems are being asked to account for the time they capture.

India does not have to copy either model. But it can learn from both. From the United States, it can see that self-regulation has limits and that piecemeal litigation is slow. From China, it can see that regulatory will can change product defaults quickly, even if the costs to speech and privacy may be unacceptable under India's constitutional framework.

<h2 id="regulation-as-a-shared-experiment">Regulation as a Shared Experiment</h2>

<span id="claim-004" class="claim-marker" data-claim="claim-004">Claim C4</span> India's regulatory choices—DSA-style due diligence, app-store rules, data protection—will shape how the global South addresses extraction.

India's regulatory choices are being watched. The Digital Personal Data Protection Act, 2023, the revised Information Technology intermediary rules with due-diligence obligations, and app-store policies are early attempts to define responsibility for platform design. These rules borrow from the European Union's Digital Services Act—risk assessments, transparency reports, grievance mechanisms—but adapt them to India's capacity and constitutional framework.

If they work, they become a template for the global South. If they are weak, captured by industry, or unevenly enforced, they become a cautionary tale. The point is not that India will solve the global attention economy on its own. The point is that India's scale means its choices matter beyond its borders.
