## Summary

Cal Newport's *Deep Work* argues that the ability to focus without distraction on cognitively demanding tasks is becoming both rare and economically valuable. The book distinguishes between "deep work," which creates new value and improves skill, and "shallow work," which is logistical or administrative and often performed while distracted. Newport emphasizes that deep work requires rituals, boundaries, and protected blocks of time, but also that these blocks can be shorter and more frequent than people assume.

## Relevance to claims

- claim-001: Supports the idea that fragmented time in a worker's day represents recoverable attention budget.
- claim-002: Reinforces that regular, protected sessions matter more than sporadic bursts for building rare and valuable skills.
- claim-003: Suggests that demonstrable deep-work output is a stronger signal than credentials in knowledge-work fields.

## Key snippet

> "The ability to perform deep work is becoming increasingly rare at exactly the same time it is becoming increasingly valuable in our economy."
