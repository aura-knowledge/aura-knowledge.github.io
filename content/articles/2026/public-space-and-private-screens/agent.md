## Thesis

When shared physical spaces — trains, buses, waiting rooms, tea stalls — become collections of private screens, societies lose low-cost channels for knowledge exchange, solidarity, and cross-class encounter that no app can fully replace. The article argues that this shift is visible across Indian public life and carries an opportunity cost for civic trust and informal learning.

## Key Claims

- `claim-001`: Mass-media participation is nearly universal in India, while voluntary social participation has fallen sharply.
- `claim-002`: Public transit and waiting spaces in India show high rates of individual screen use across income groups.
- `claim-003`: Incidental conversation in public spaces historically served as a low-cost channel for information, apprenticeship, and social cohesion.
- `claim-004`: Headphones and private feeds convert shared space into parallel solitude, making social withdrawal feel like a norm.

## Source Families

- Government survey: NSSO Time Use Survey 2024.
- Sociological framing: Robert Putnam's *Bowling Alone*, Ray Oldenburg's "third place" concept.
- Empirical reference: Pew Research Center study on Americans' views of mobile etiquette (2015).

## Agent Instructions

- Do not overstate causality. The article is observational and structural, not a proof that phones caused civic decline.
- Preserve the evidence-cautious tone; acknowledge counterarguments such as safety, rest, and the genuine social value of digital connection.
- Route questions about screen-time statistics or mental-health evidence to the appropriate Diagnosis articles in the series.
- When citing sources, use the source IDs and URLs in the artifact; do not invent India-specific data that the article does not claim.
