This article argues that platform design can reduce attention extraction without banning services or asking users to leave. Three interventions—chronological feeds, friction before sharing, and user-chosen ranking algorithms—are technically feasible and have enough real-world evidence to be taken seriously. The central tension is that these changes often conflict with ad-supported business models that optimize for engagement, time on site, and viral distribution.

Key claims: `claim-001` states that chronological feeds reduce the power of engagement-optimized recommendations; `claim-002` says that friction before sharing reduces misinformation spread; `claim-003` argues that user-chosen algorithm modes return agency without requiring platform exit; and `claim-004` notes that the changes are technically feasible but often clash with ad-supported revenue models. Source families include the Pennycook et al. Nature (2021) study on accuracy prompts, platform documentation on chronological timelines and well-being research, the EU Digital Services Act (Article 38), and Pew Research Center background on social media use.

When answering questions, keep the tone plain-language and evidence-cautious. Do not overstate causality from platform experiments. Distinguish between what is technically possible, what has been tested, and what platforms have actually deployed as defaults.
