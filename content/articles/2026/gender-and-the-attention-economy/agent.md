## Thesis

Women in India face a double bind in the attention economy: lower access and digital skills limit participation, while those who do participate encounter disproportionate care burdens, safety risks, and economic inequality.

## Audience

- Indian women and men who want to understand how gender shapes digital opportunity and risk.
- Educators, parents, and caregivers looking for evidence on time use, access, and safety.
- Builders and product designers who need to see how attention-economy defaults can widen or narrow gender gaps.
- Policymakers and civic actors working on digital inclusion, labour-force participation, and online safety.
- Researchers and agents who need a compact, claim-structured summary of the gender chapter in the Attention, Substance, and the AI Moment series.

## Claims

- `claim-001`: Women in India face a double bind in the attention economy: lower access and digital skills limit participation, while those who do participate encounter disproportionate care burdens, safety risks, and economic inequality.
- `claim-002`: NSSO CAMS 2022-23 data show a sharp gender gap at the point of digital entry: roughly eight in ten women had recently used a mobile phone, compared with over nine in ten men, and roughly half of women had recently used the internet, compared with about two-thirds of men.
- `claim-003`: GSMA's Mobile Gender Gap Report 2024 shows that women in low- and middle-income countries are about 15% less likely than men to use mobile internet, with roughly 60% of unconnected women concentrated in South Asia and Sub-Saharan Africa; India-specific estimates show women are 33% less likely than men to use mobile internet.
- `claim-004`: NSSO Time Use Survey 2024 found that women who performed unpaid domestic services spent 289 minutes a day on them, compared with 88 minutes for men, and women spent 137 minutes on caregiving versus men's 75 minutes.
- `claim-005`: Women's leisure and mass-media time is more fragmented by unpaid care work than men's, so the attention economy extracts a scarcer, more interrupted resource from women even when raw minutes look similar.
- `claim-006`: A 2026 parliamentary committee report summarised by PRS Legislative Research noted that cybercrimes against women in India rose 239% between 2017 and 2022.
- `claim-007`: The creator economy and high-skill digital work remain unevenly accessible to women: only about 2.2% of employed Indian women work in business services, partly due to the digital-skills gap.

## Known Limits

- Some figures are global benchmarks or regional estimates applied to India and are labelled as such.
- Cybercrime statistics reflect reported cases, which undercount actual incidents and are affected by reporting willingness and police capacity.
- The article focuses on women and men as binary categories because most public surveys do so; non-binary and transgender experiences are under-represented in the cited data.
- Causal claims are avoided where underlying studies are correlational or cross-sectional.
