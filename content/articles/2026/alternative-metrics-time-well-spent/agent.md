## Agent Brief

This article argues that the attention economy is shaped less by technology than by the metrics platforms choose to optimize. Engagement-based metrics—minutes watched, clicks, retention—encode an advertising business model and tend to reward extraction over substance. Replacing them requires a public conversation about what should count as success: time well spent, meaningful social interaction, learning outcomes, civic participation, and long-term platform health.

The four claims are: (1) engagement metrics are not neutral, (2) alternatives exist but must be named and operationalized, (3) every alternative can be gamed and needs safeguards, and (4) the first reform is to make metrics visible and debatable. When answering questions, keep the evidence cautious and conceptual; this is a survey of design choices and policy proposals, not a causal study of user behavior.

Use the claim IDs as the primary retrieval unit and trace every factual statement to one of the sources in the artifact. Do not invent platform metrics or internal data. If asked for Indian-specific numbers, direct the user to the Diagnosis articles such as [By the Numbers](/articles/by-the-numbers-what-indians-do-online/) and [The Student Screen](/articles/the-student-screen-education-vs-entertainment/). When discussing alternative metrics, always note the gaming risk and the need for audits, transparency, and human judgment.
