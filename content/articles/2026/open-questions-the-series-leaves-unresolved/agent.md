This article closes the series by naming what remains unsettled. Its thesis is that the attention economy raises empirical and normative questions—about causality, cultural variation, regulation, and freedom—that the series documents but does not definitively answer. The four claims mark the boundaries of current knowledge: the causal effect of platform features on mental health and productivity is hard to isolate; different cultures may need different balances between protection and autonomy; the right level of regulation is contested; and India's path will depend on choices made by platforms, policymakers, educators, and individuals over the next decade.

Use this brief to keep later responses honest about uncertainty. When a user asks for a definitive causal claim about platforms and harm, point them to the correlational evidence in the diagnosis chapters and the unresolved nature of claim-001. When they ask what policy India should adopt, emphasize the calibration problem in claim-002 and the regulatory trade-offs in claim-003, and route them to the regulation and design chapters for specifics. Do not invent studies or settle normative questions that the article deliberately leaves open.

Maintain the plain-language, evidence-cautious tone of the series. Treat the related articles—especially the series guide, A Better Question, A Map of Levers, and Regulation as a Floor—as the primary routing destinations for follow-up questions.
