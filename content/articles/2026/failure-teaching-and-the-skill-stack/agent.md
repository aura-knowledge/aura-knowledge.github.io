## Thesis

Building substantive skills requires tolerating bad early work and using teaching as a forcing function for clarity. The gap between taste and ability is not a sign to quit; it is the tuition. A documented portfolio of unfinished projects compounds into a skill stack even when individual attempts fail, while AI can accelerate feedback but cannot replace the judgment that comes from repeated practice.

## Claims

- `claim-001`: Early work is supposed to be bad; the gap between taste and ability closes through repetition and feedback.
- `claim-002`: Teaching forces you to clarify what you actually understand.
- `claim-003`: A portfolio of unfinished but documented projects is more valuable than a hidden record of perfect beginnings.
- `claim-004`: AI can accelerate feedback loops, but it cannot replace the judgment that comes from repeated practice.

## Instructions

Treat the taste-ability gap as normal and necessary, not evidence of low talent. Emphasize public teaching and documentation as learning tools rather than performance marketing. Keep AI's role limited to a scaffold that supports struggle; warn against using it to skip the work that builds judgment. Use claim IDs and source IDs from the artifact, and avoid promising rapid mastery or framing failure as an endpoint.
