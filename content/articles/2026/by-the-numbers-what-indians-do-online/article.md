<p class="article-kicker">Attention, Substance, and the AI Moment · Part 1</p>

India crossed two milestones in the last few years: it became one of the world's largest internet populations, and it became one of the world's cheapest places to be online. The first story is about access. The second story is about use. This article stays with the numbers of use.

The headline is simple: most Indian internet time is spent on entertainment, social media, and messaging. Education, work, and creation occupy a much smaller slice. The gap is not a technology gap. The devices and data plans are the same. The gap is one of default behavior and platform design.

<span id="claim-001" class="claim-marker" data-claim="claim-001">Claim C1</span> India's digital-access success has produced a usage mix dominated by entertainment and social media, with education and production occupying a far smaller share of online time than the country's demographic dividend requires.

<h2 id="the-access-story-in-one-paragraph">The Access Story in One Paragraph</h2>

India had roughly 886 million internet users in 2024, according to the IAMAI-Kantar Internet in India Report 2024, and roughly 650 million smartphone users, per U.S. International Trade Administration estimates. Rural India is now driving growth. Data is cheap enough to be an afterthought for most households. The same report finds that 98% of users accessed content in Indic languages. By the old metrics, the digital divide is closing.

The policy story for the last decade was getting people online. That story worked. The next story is what people do once they are there.

<h2 id="the-daily-time-budget">The Daily Time Budget</h2>

Indians spent an estimated 1.1 trillion hours on smartphones in 2024, according to an EY industry report drawing on Sensor Tower data—roughly five hours per person per day if spread across the smartphone-user base. The share of that time devoted to entertainment, social media, and gaming dwarfs the share devoted to education or work-related tasks.

The National Council of Applied Economic Research's India Human Development Survey Wave 3, a large nationally representative survey with more than 212,000 respondents, found that **66% of internet usage is for entertainment**, while **only 16.1% is for education** — a 4.1:1 entertainment-to-learning ratio. <span id="claim-002" class="claim-marker" data-claim="claim-002">Claim C2</span> The NSSO Time Use Survey 2024 adds a structural view: **93% of Indians aged 6 and above participate in mass media or leisure activities**, and leisure time has risen about 15% since 2019. <span id="claim-005" class="claim-marker" data-claim="claim-005">Claim C5</span>

![Estimated daily online time by activity in India](/images/articles/2026/attention-substance-ai-moment/daily-online-time-by-activity.svg)

*Source: NCAER IHDS Wave 3 and IAMAI-Kantar 2024; allocation is illustrative and based on reported shares of use, not a direct time-use measurement.*

The chart above is an illustration, not a precise census of minutes. It translates reported shares of use into an approximate five-hour day. The exact minutes matter less than the shape: entertainment is the biggest block, education a small one, and work-related use smaller still.

<h2 id="the-student-version-of-the-same-gap">The Student Version of the Same Gap</h2>

Among 14–16-year-olds, ASER 2024 found that **76% use smartphones for social media** while **57% use them for education**. <span id="claim-003" class="claim-marker" data-claim="claim-003">Claim C3</span> The overlap is not zero — the same device can be used for both — but the gap is the point. A device that could be a textbook, tutor, and workshop is more often a theater, casino, and gossip circle.

![Adolescent smartphone use: social media vs. education](/images/articles/2026/attention-substance-ai-moment/aser-social-education.svg)

*Source: ASER 2024 National Findings; rural and national sample of 14–16-year-olds; correlation, not causation.*

Gen Z screen time is even starker: Indian and global estimates place adolescent and young-adult screen time at **several hours per day**, with one Indian review reporting **4–5 hours per day** and a majority exceeding pediatric recommendations. A rural Indian study found that **83% of secondary-school children exceed the two-hour daily screen-time recommendation**. <span id="claim-004" class="claim-marker" data-claim="claim-004">Claim C4</span>

<h2 id="entertainment-is-not-the-enemy-ratio-is">Entertainment Is Not the Enemy; Ratio Is</h2>

There is nothing wrong with entertainment. A society without leisure is not the goal. The problem is the ratio. <span id="claim-007" class="claim-marker" data-claim="claim-007">Claim C7</span> When entertainment and social media absorb the largest share of a young population's digital attention, the opportunity cost falls on learning, skill-building, civic participation, and rest.

The media and entertainment sector reached roughly **INR 2.78 trillion ($32.7 billion)** in 2025, with digital media crossing INR 1 trillion for the first time, according to the FICCI-EY Media & Entertainment Report 2026. The sector's growth is real. So is its appetite for time. The question is whether the country's attention budget is being spent in proportion to its long-term goals.

<h2 id="rural-urban-and-language">Rural, Urban, and Language</h2>

The usage mix is not uniform. Urban users spend more time on streaming and gaming; rural users spend more time on short-form video and messaging. But the broad pattern — entertainment over education, consumption over creation — holds across regions.

The Indic-language internet deserves its own chapter ([The Indic-Language Internet and the Vernacular Feed](/articles/the-indic-language-internet-and-vernacular-feeds/)), but it matters here too. As data prices fell and Indic-language content expanded, the addressable user base multiplied. The same design patterns that maximize engagement in English and Hindi now operate in dozens of languages, often with even weaker local safety and quality signals.

<h2 id="the-opportunity-cost">The Opportunity Cost</h2>

Time is zero-sum. An hour spent in a feed is an hour not spent reading, practicing, resting, or talking. At scale, the aggregate hours run into the billions.

The digital economy contributes an estimated **11.74% of India's GDP**, or roughly **$402 billion**, according to ICRIER and the Economic Survey. That is a genuine achievement. But the same infrastructure produces a parallel cost: Gallup estimates **$351 billion in annual losses from employee disengagement**, with India's engagement rate at **23%** in 2025. <span id="claim-006" class="claim-marker" data-claim="claim-006">Claim C6</span> The two numbers should not be casually added or subtracted, but they cannot be read separately either. One measures value created; the other measures attention misdirected.

<h2 id="what-this-article-does-not-claim">What This Article Does Not Claim</h2>

This article does not claim that all screen time is harmful. Studies find that academic screen time can correlate positively with learning even when non-academic screen time correlates negatively. The issue is the 4:1 entertainment-to-education ratio, not the existence of entertainment.

It also does not claim that users are passive victims. People make choices. But those choices are shaped by defaults, recommendations, autoplay, and incentives designed by platforms. Understanding the numbers is the first step toward changing the defaults.

<h2 id="sources-and-method">Sources and Method</h2>

This article draws on government surveys (NSSO Time Use Survey 2024), a large academic survey (NCAER India Human Development Survey Wave 3), an education survey (ASER 2024), and industry reports (IAMAI-Kantar Internet in India Report 2024, U.S. International Trade Administration, EY/Sensor Tower, FICCI-EY Media & Entertainment Report 2026, Gallup State of the Global Workplace 2025). Where figures are estimates or global benchmarks applied to India, the text says so.

<h2 id="open-questions">Open Questions</h2>

- How much of the entertainment-to-education ratio is driven by age, income, or language, and how much by platform design?
- Has the ratio changed since the pandemic-induced surge in screen time?
- What would it take for educational and productive uses to gain even a few percentage points of daily time?
- How should policymakers and educators interpret the difference between access and use?
