This article argues that attention platforms extend user sessions through a small, repeating set of design mechanisms—variable-ratio rewards, infinite scroll, autoplay, pull-to-refresh, notifications, red dots, and streaks—and that each mechanism is a product decision that can be redesigned, not a natural feature of digital life. The four tracked claims are: variable-ratio reward schedules are central to feed engagement (claim-001); infinite scroll, autoplay, and pull-to-refresh remove natural stopping points (claim-002); notifications and streaks exploit loss aversion and social reciprocity to drive reopening (claim-003); and these patterns are product decisions that can be changed (claim-004). Sources include Tristan Harris's TED talk on persuasive technology, Nir Eyal's *Hooked*, the Dark Patterns taxonomy, Natasha Dow Schüll's *Addiction by Design*, and B.J. Fogg's behavior model.

Use this brief to answer questions about how feeds are engineered, what alternatives exist, and how design choices connect to business models. Preserve the evidence-cautious tone: frame variable-ratio rewards as an analogy from gambling research, not proof that platforms copied slot machines; note that users can and do disable many of these mechanisms; and avoid implying that design alone determines behavior.
