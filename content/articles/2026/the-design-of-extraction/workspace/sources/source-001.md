## Summary

Former Google design ethicist Tristan Harris argues that a small number of technology companies shape the attention of billions through persuasive design techniques. He uses the slot-machine metaphor for the pull-to-refresh gesture and describes how design choices such as variable rewards and social-approval metrics can override user intentions.

## Relevance to claims

- claim-001: Provides the public-facing framing that connects feed uncertainty to slot-machine-like variable rewards.
- claim-004: Supports the argument that these mechanisms are design choices and that alternative designs are possible.

## Key snippet

> "There are a whole bunch of techniques that are used to keep people using the product as much as possible... It's not just about the time you spend, it's about whether you spend your life in accordance with what you care about."
