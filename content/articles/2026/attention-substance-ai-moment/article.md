<p class="article-kicker">Attention, Substance, and the AI Moment · Series Guide</p>

India built one of the world's largest digital-access stories. Cheap data, cheap phones, and Indic-language content brought hundreds of millions online. The infrastructure was built for connection. What grew on top of it was largely an attention-extraction economy: feeds, reels, games, and infinite scroll designed to capture time rather than cultivate it.

This series is an attempt to look at that inversion clearly, without shaming individuals or pretending the problem will solve itself. It asks what India—and any country moving through the same transition—stands to lose if the attention economy keeps winning, and what becomes possible if the same tools are turned toward learning, creation, and civic problem-solving.

<span id="claim-001" class="claim-marker" data-claim="claim-001">Claim C1</span> India's digital-access success has created an attention-extraction economy that diverts time and talent away from learning, creation, and civic problem-solving, but the same infrastructure could be redirected toward substance if individuals, platforms, and policymakers make different choices.

<h2 id="the-six-arcs">The Six Arcs</h2>

The series is organized into six arcs. Each arc is a self-contained thread; each article can be read on its own, but the arcs build on one another.

1. **The Diagnosis** — What the public evidence says about time use, attention, mental health, productivity, platform economics, and social trust in India and similar mobile-first countries.
2. **Historical and Human Frames** — Why access to a powerful tool does not guarantee its benefits, and why attention habits matter across a human life.
3. **The AI Opportunity Cost** — What artificial intelligence makes cheap, what India risks missing, and why the next few years are a compounding bet.
4. **Building Substance** — Practical, low-friction paths for individuals, families, students, workers, and citizens to turn dead time into small acts of creation and learning.
5. **Designing for Substance** — How platforms, metrics, regulation, and alternative business models could make substance the easier path.
6. **Synthesis and Action** — How the threads fit together, who can pull which levers, and what questions remain unresolved.

<h2 id="a-note-on-evidence">A Note on Evidence</h2>

The series draws on four kinds of public sources:

- **Government surveys and releases**, including ASER, NSSO Time Use Survey, NCERT and Tele-MANAS reports, PIB releases, and the Economic Survey.
- **Peer-reviewed and academic studies** on attention, short-form video, mental health, and platform behavior.
- **Industry and business reports** on internet use, advertising, creator economics, and AI adoption.
- **International sources** for comparison, including the OECD, Stanford HAI, ITU, GSMA, and regulatory texts such as the EU Digital Services Act.

Every claim is framed with appropriate caution. Where the evidence is correlational, the language reflects that. The goal is not to prove a single causal chain but to show that enough public evidence points in the same direction to treat attention extraction as a serious structural problem—and to treat the alternative as a real possibility.
